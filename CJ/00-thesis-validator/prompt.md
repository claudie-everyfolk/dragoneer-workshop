I co-lead private investments at a firm that writes mid-to-late-stage growth checks across software, fintech, and vertical AI. A lot of my week is spent on expert calls — former operators, channel partners, departing executives — trying to figure out whether the innovation tailwind around a theme is real, AND whether the specific companies we own (or are looking at) are actually positioned to ride it.

The trap I keep falling into: an expert tells a great story about a theme, and I let the narrative pull me past the question of whether OUR portfolio company is really the one capturing the tailwind. Theme-chasing. I want to systematize the discipline of separating "the theme is real" from "this company benefits from the theme."

I have three sources I want you to synthesize. An expert call transcript with a former GC at a large enterprise (Helena Park) talking about vertical AI for legal workflows. A CSV of our current portfolio companies in adjacent themes, with KPIs and revenue trajectory. A markdown file of external signals — recent press, hiring trends, search-volume data — on the vertical-AI-for-legal theme.

Using the attached sample data files — `expert_call_helena_park.md`, `portfolio_snapshot.csv`, `external_signals_vertical_ai_legal.md` — build me a **Thesis Validation Memo** that includes:

1. **Theme Assumption** — In 2-3 sentences, state the underlying thesis the expert call is implicitly arguing for. Be precise about what would have to be true.
2. **Expert Signal Decomposition** — Pull out 4-6 specific claims Helena made. For each: classify as Confirmation, Contradiction, or Mixed signal for the theme. Quote or tightly paraphrase the line that supports the classification.
3. **Portfolio Reality Check** — For each portfolio company in the CSV that touches this theme, compare expert-stated tailwinds against the company's actual KPIs and revenue trajectory. Where is the company catching the wave? Where is it lagging? Where is the data ambiguous?
4. **External Signal Cross-Check** — Use the external signals file to either reinforce or pressure-test what the expert said. Flag any divergence between expert narrative and outside-in data (hiring trends, search velocity, press tone).
5. **Revised Conviction** — A 3-tier output: companies I should LEAN INTO, companies I should HOLD/WATCH, companies where the thesis looks like theme-chasing. For each, one sentence on why.
6. **Disconfirming Questions** — Three sharp questions I should ask on my next expert call to actively try to break this thesis (not reinforce it).

Output as a single clean markdown memo I could send to my co-head. Be willing to disagree with the expert if the portfolio data or external signals don't back her up — that's the whole point.

This matters because most of my expert-call workflow today is one-way: expert says something interesting, I get excited, I write it into a memo, the memo gets pushed into IC. The missing step is the gating function — does the rest of the evidence actually support the story? If you can connect Helena's qualitative claims to our portfolio KPIs and to outside-in signals, you're doing the work that currently lives only in my head and only gets done when I have time.

**Iteration hooks:**
- After the first pass, ask me which companies I want to go deeper on, then expand the Portfolio Reality Check for those specifically.
- If any expert claim feels ambiguous, surface it explicitly — don't paper over it.
- Push back on me if I tell you to soften a contradiction. I'd rather see the conflict than read a clean memo.
